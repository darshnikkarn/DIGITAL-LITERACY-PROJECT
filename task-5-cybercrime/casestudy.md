Case Study: University Network Ransomware Attack

Ransomware is a type of malicious software designed to block access to a computer system or files until a sum of money is paid. In a typical scenario, a cybercriminal targets an institution, such as a university, which relies heavily on accessible data.

The attack usually unfolds in several distinct steps. First, the attacker infiltrates the network, often through a phishing email disguised as an urgent communication from the university administration or IT department. Once a student or staff member unknowingly clicks the malicious link or downloads the infected attachment, the ransomware silently installs itself. Second, the software rapidly spreads across the connected network, scanning for valuable files, research data, and student records. Third, the ransomware encrypts these files using complex cryptographic algorithms, rendering them entirely inaccessible. Finally, a ransom note appears on infected screens, demanding payment—usually in cryptocurrency—in exchange for the decryption key.

Universities and healthcare systems are frequent targets because their critical need for immediate data access makes them more likely to pay. The consequences of such an attack are devastating. For a university, it means the immediate halt of academic operations, loss of years of vital research, compromised personal data of thousands of students, and severe financial and reputational damage. Even if the ransom is paid, there is never a guarantee that the cybercriminals will actually restore access to the encrypted data.
